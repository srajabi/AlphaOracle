### 1. Geopolitical Landscape: Tectonic Shift in Oil Risk Premium

**Developments:**
- Multiple major outlets (WSJ, NYT, Reuters) report a **rapid de-escalation** in the Strait of Hormuz standoff. Brent crude has fallen to its lowest since before the Iran war began (~$74, first time below that threshold). The US has cleared the way for Iran to sell oil in dollars, and Trump claims record volumes transited Monday.
- The peace process is accelerating; US-Iran deal hopes are driving the narrative. The market is pricing out much of the pre-war supply-risk premium in oil.

**Second-order effects:**
- **Broken trade:** The bulk of our high-conviction "geopolitical supply shock" thesis for XLE and energy hedging is now unwinding. Energy assets had been a structural overweight in many defensive sleeves; this is a clear signal to reduce that tilt.
- **Inflation implications:** Oil down → CPI relief → the Fed’s cornered position (cannot hike into war, cannot cut into 4.2% CPI) becomes slightly less constrained. The hawkish Fed tone from Chair Warsh may actually gain credibility if inflation prints begin to ease. This is a slow-moving positive for equities over 6-12 months but negative for gold and long-duration TLT as a hedge (TLT already showed a minor uptick today, but that seems more a safe-haven flow than a rates-driven rally).
- **Sector rotation:** The easing of supply fears is crushing energy stocks (XLE -7.8% momentum, near BB lower). Money is rotating out of energy and into areas that benefit from lower input costs: transports, industrials, discretionary. The fact that tech is also selling off on AI spending concerns means the rotation is **not a simple risk-on shift**—it is a **sectoral rotation within a cautious environment**.

**Second-order risk:** If Hormuz peace is fully realized, the other geopolitical tail (China-Taiwan, trade policy) may come into sharper focus. China recently imposed trade curbs on dozens of US firms, and the Taiwan semiconductor angle remains live. That could cap the breadth of any risk-on rally.

### 2. Fed & Rates: Hawkish Stance Meets Easing Geopolitics

**Key signals:**
- BofA predicts a series of rate hikes starting this year; JPMorgan warns July 29 is live for a hike. The market is pricing higher rates.
- Yet the 10-year yield (^TNX) has fallen slightly today (4.45% vs 20-day SMA 4.49%). TLT is up ~0.7% from its 20-day SMA. **This divergence is critical**: bond yields are declining *despite* hawkish Fed rhetoric. That implies the market is reading the oil price drop as a disinflationary force that may allow the Fed to hold fire. It also suggests growth fears (softening labor data, recession signals) are competing with inflation fears.

- S&P 500 (SPY) is still above its 200-day SMA but below its 20-day SMA. The VIX at 19.5 (above the 17.6 median) indicates elevated uncertainty.

**Takeaway:** The rates complex is caught between a hawkish Fed (hikes) and disinflationary tailwinds (falling oil). This is a classic **stagflation tug-of-war**. TLT is not a clean hedge here—its movement is driven more by growth expectations than by rate expectations. The canary signals (EWA, TLT momentum positive) suggest the bond rally may be driven by safe-haven buying, not a bullish economic outlook.
