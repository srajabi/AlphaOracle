# A Census of the Factor Zoo

**Citation:** Harvey, C. R., & Liu, Y. (2019). *A Census of the Factor Zoo.*
Working paper.

**Links:** [SSRN 3341728](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3341728)
| Companion Google Sheet of 400+ factors linked from the paper/Harvey's
[Duke page](https://people.duke.edu/~charvey/)
| Found via r/quant "papers that translate into strategies" thread.

## Claims

- Catalogs every factor published in top journals through 2019: 400+
  documented return factors, with citations and data links in a maintained
  Google Sheet (a "citizen science" census).
- Companion to Harvey-Liu-Zhu's *"...and the Cross-Section of Expected
  Returns"*: given how many factors have been mined, a newly proposed factor
  should clear a t-stat > 3.0 (not the usual 2.0) to be credible.
- Most published factors are false discoveries or decay sharply
  post-publication (see also McLean & Pontiff 2016: ~58% decay).

## Criticisms

- A census, not a verdict - it doesn't itself test which factors survive.
- The t>3 threshold is itself debated (Bayesian alternatives exist).

## AlphaOracle verdict

A **meta-source**, not a strategy: the sheet is a shopping catalog for ideas,
and the multiple-testing message is the same one our
[deflated Sharpe note](deflated_sharpe.md) carries - with 400+ published
factors, the prior on any single one being real is low.

Practical use for us: when a new factor/strategy idea shows up (Reddit,
vince.quant, arXiv), check whether it's in the census and what its
publication date and journal are - post-publication decay means newer +
flashier = less trustworthy. Our lab's focus on simple, decades-old,
economically-motivated rules (trend, momentum breadth, vol scaling) is the
defensible corner of the zoo.
