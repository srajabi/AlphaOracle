1. Deploying Cash Near Market Highs

Question: a large cash position needs to enter the market, but the market looks toppy. Lump sum? Dollar-cost average? Wait for a dip? Use a trend signal?

Method: every entry rule was simulated from every monthly start date with a 5-year horizon (SPY 1993–2021 starts, 328 windows; 60/40 SPY/TLT 2003–2021 starts). Cash earns 3%/yr while undeployed. “At the top” = the start date was within 2% of the prior all-time high — the scenario that feels scariest. Wealth = ending value per $1 after 5 years. Worst max DD = deepest account drawdown across all start dates in the cohort.

Key findings

  • Lump sum wins the median, even at all-time highs. This matches the published research (Morgan Stanley, Vanguard: lump sum beats DCA in 56–75% of periods).
  • DCA barely protects the tail. Spreading entries over 6–24 months still ends with a worst-case drawdown of -50%+ on equities — once fully invested, the crash hits you anyway. DCA is mostly a psychological tool.
  • Waiting for a dip is the worst rule. The 10%-dip rule has the lowest median wealth: markets often run 20%+ before the dip arrives, and the dip you finally get may be the start of something worse.
  • What actually truncates the left tail is the ongoing SMA200 overlay, not the entry schedule. On equities at tops: worst case 0.94x vs 0.82x and max drawdown capped near -22% vs -55%, costing ~0.14x median wealth.
  • On a 60/40 portfolio the overlay is close to free. At-the-top entries: worst 5-year outcome was +21%, worst drawdown -11% (vs -30%), and it beat lump sum 53% of the time. For money that cannot tolerate deep drawdowns (e.g. retirement funds), allocation choice + trend overlay matters far more than entry timing.

2. Leveraged ETF Strategy Comparison

Question: for an aggressive sleeve of the portfolio, which leveraged ETF strategy from the r/LETFs playbook holds up best across regimes?

Method: 33 years (1993–2026), zero transaction fees, clean leverage (3x daily index returns). Important: clean leverage ignores ~1% expense ratio and financing drag (roughly 2× the T-bill rate for a 3x fund — at 4% short rates that is ~8%/yr). Key findings

  • TQQQ buy & hold is uninvestable: -94% max drawdown (GFC). The CAGR looks similar to the 200 SMA version, but nobody holds through -94%.
  • The 200 SMA filter is what makes leveraged equity survivable: it turned the GFC from -94% into -36% on TQQQ, with only ~0.7 trades/year.
  • HFEA died in 2022 exactly as its critics predicted: -62% in one year because stocks and long bonds crashed together when rates rose. Its 33-year numbers are carried by a 30-year bond bull market that is not guaranteed to repeat.
  • No timing signal saves you from a gap-crash: COVID hit faster than the 200 SMA could react (-61% for TQQQ+SMA during the crash window). The SMA filter protects against slow bears (2000, 2008, 2022), not fast ones.
  • Regime rotation 2x had the best risk-adjusted profile (Sharpe 0.89, -47% max DD) but trades 22x/year — only suitable for tax-sheltered accounts.
  • These strategies excelled mostly in 2011+ bull markets. The same SMA200 rule lost money on TQQQ from 1993–2010. Regime dependency is the biggest risk.

3. Does SMA200 Timing Work on XEQT / VEQT?

Question: SMA200 looks great on the TQQQ forward test — should the same rule manage a diversified all-equity fund (XEQT/VEQT)?

Short answer: no. Timing earns its whipsaw costs on leveraged funds where drawdowns are existential. On unleveraged diversified funds it mostly locks in whipsaw losses.

VT 2010+ (fair test, signal live throughout) CAGRSharpeMax DDTrades
Buy & hold9.8%0.56-34.2%1
SMA200 in/out6.4%0.56-19.0%99
SMA200 with 5%/3% bands5.3%0.47-24.1%21

Key findings

  • SMA200 gave up a third of CAGR for an identical Sharpe. The only benefit is a shallower max drawdown (-19% vs -34%).
  • Watch out for the warmup artifact in short backtests: the naive XEQT/VEQT backtest (2019+) flattered SMA200 because the signal sat in cash during its 200-day warmup — which happened to cover the COVID crash. Skill was actually luck.
  • In taxable accounts it is strictly worse: every SMA exit realizes capital gains. Inside TFSA/RRSP the rule costs return but no tax.
  • Verdict: hold XEQT/VEQT as buy-and-hold; if -30% drawdowns are intolerable, fix the allocation (add bonds), not the timing.

4. “Beyond the Status Quo” — Should Retirees Hold 100% Equities?

The paper: Anarkulova, Cederburg & O'Doherty (2023) — often called the most controversial paper in finance — argues a fixed ~33/67 domestic/international all-equity portfolio beats every stock/bond lifecycle strategy including in retirement, and that target-date fund investors need 63% more savings for the same retirement outcome. Method: block-bootstrap over ~2,500 country-years across 38 developed markets.

The pushback: the paper's own US-only table shows 60/40 with a lower ruin rate than 100% stocks (4.5% vs ~5%); the bootstrap mixes in post-war Germany/Japan return sequences; real retirement cohorts (1972, 2000, 2007) did better with bonds; the worst equity crashes were deflationary (when bonds shine); only static allocations were compared; and it assumes retirees hold through 50%+ drawdowns without capitulating.

Our test: 4% rule, 25-year retirements, SPY (starts 1993–2001)

Every start window below lived through BOTH the dot-com crash and the GFC. Windows overlap heavily — treat as illustrative, not independent samples. Withdrawals are 4%/yr of the starting balance, inflation-adjusted at 3%. Ending balances are in real (start-date) dollars.

Key findings

  • Both sides are right at their own horizon. Equities dominate long horizons (10y at-top median 2.53x vs 60/40's 2.26x) — the paper's core logic holds. But a 2000-top all-equity entry was still underwater ten years later (0.72x), and a retiree withdrawing through that does not recover.
  • Our ruin test matches the paper's US numbers (~4.5% all-equity ruin) and its critics' point: the 5th-percentile retiree ended with ~1% of their starting wealth.
  • The SMA200 overlay answered the behavioral critique: zero ruin across windows containing two 50% crashes, drawdowns capped at -31% (vs -64%), at the cost of lower median ending wealth for at-top retirees (1.42x vs 1.89x).
  • Synthesis for real retirement money: bucket by horizon. The paper's logic for the 10+ year bucket (more equity than old-school glide paths), cash/bonds for years 0–5 of spending, and a trend overlay on the equity sleeve as the disaster brake. No single static allocation wins both games.
  • Caveats: US-only data (the paper's international claim is untested here); ~88 overlapping windows are far fewer independent samples; the overlay has whipsaw and tax costs; 2026 bonds at positive real yields differ from the 2021 bonds the paper's critics were defending.
